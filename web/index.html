AI NHI Analysis — Plain English

Why we built Resilience

Most self-defense advice comes from gym marketing, action movies, or one famous fight from 1993. People spend years learning Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu or Krav Maga without anyone showing them what the numbers actually say.

Resilience is an open research project built from first principles — not gym tradition. We asked: What must be true for a human to survive predatory violence? Then we rebuilt recommendations from physics, biology, and crime data only. Three deliberately disagreeing reviewers scored everything using 3×7 adversarial voting — so no single bias could win.

Important: This is educational research, not a training program. It will not make you invincible. It will help you stop preparing for the wrong kind of danger.

"So is this an AI telling me how to fight?"

No. This is a research summary that explains what human studies measured — and what they did not measure. The biggest finding is that the highest win rate is not fighting at all. It is noticing danger early and leaving before fists fly.

"What's NHI?"

Non-Human Intelligence — meaning this analysis was synthesized by AI from primary sources, then stress-tested by adversarial review rules so conclusions are not just one chatbot's opinion. Every claim on this page ties back to a source in our GitHub data files.

Five things that win most often

1

Detect danger and leave

The EAAA program taught women to spot risky situations earlier and set firm boundaries. Result: 50–57% fewer completed assaults in medical trials. Only ~2 hours of the 12-hour course is physical fighting. The rest is awareness and confidence.

2

Stay alert by default Yellow

Walk through life in "relaxed alert" (Cooper's Yellow): phone down, exits noted, gut feelings respected. No percentage proves this alone — but every downstream stat shows people who engage late lose more often.

3

Run when outnumbered

If you're already facing multiple attackers: 22% escaped vs 11% fought their way out (154 street fights studied). Escape is twice as likely to work as fighting.

4

Simple punches, first 10 seconds

If you must fight one person standing up: boxing-style jab, cross, hook. 64% of knockouts in street fights happened in the first 10 seconds. Fancy kicks and movie moves rarely show up in real footage.

5

Get up — don't grapple on pavement

Ground fighting happens a lot in street fights — but against concrete, with friends jumping in, it is dangerous. If you go down, stand up and create distance. Sport grappling is for sport and police control — not bar fights.

What Is EAAA? — Empowerment Self-Defense Explained

EAAA (Enhanced Assess, Acknowledge, Act) is the only self-defense approach on this site with a medical-trial proof of fewer assaults. Empowerment Self-Defense (ESD) is the broader field — confidence, boundaries, awareness, and physical options. EAAA is the research-validated program inside that family.

EAAA Curriculum Split (12 hours)

"Is every empowerment self-defense class the same as EAAA?"

No. Many studios use the words "empowerment" or "women's self-defense" without the SARE curriculum or outcome data. EAAA is a specific 12-hour program developed by Dr. Charlene Senn and tested in a randomized trial. This site's AAA Exit Protocol borrows EAAA's logic (Assess · Acknowledge · Act) in a 3-minute daily habit — not a certificate that you completed EAAA.

"Why does Resilience rank it above boxing and BJJ?"

Because the win condition for civilians is leaving unharmed, and EAAA is the only system with an RCT showing ~50–57% fewer completed assaults. Boxing proves delivery in fights; EAAA proves fewer assaults completed — a different and higher-leverage outcome for ordinary life.

The OODA Loop — How Decisions Happen Under Pressure

Col. John Boyd's Observe → Orient → Decide → Act cycle explains why surprised defenders lose time — and why awareness, rehearsal, and early exit beat technique menus. Tier C as a framework; pairs with EAAA (A+) and AAA habits.

OODA cycle visualization

"If OODA says act fast, shouldn't I train more punches?"

Boyd's point is tempo — not a bigger technique list. Under surprise, your full loop takes 500–1500ms+. A committed punch lands sooner. The highest-tempo winning Act for civilians is usually leave before Red, or a pre-rehearsed verbal boundary that forces the aggressor to re-Orient. Punches are backup after avoidance fails.

"How is OODA different from Cooper colors?"

Cooper is your alertness setting (White→Red). OODA is the decision machinery running inside that setting. Yellow Cooper means better Observe; Orange means Orient+Decide activating; Red means Act. They stack, not compete.

How We Scored Everything (3×7 Adversarial Voting)

Instead of one opinion, we ran three reviewers who argue on purpose — a fight-sport expert, a police-data expert, and a skeptic who demands medical-trial proof. They scored 7 approaches on 7 questions. Proactive avoidance counted most.

What is 3×7 adversarial voting? (Read this first)

Plain English: Imagine three expert judges who are told to disagree — then forced to defend every score with real sources. That is this method.

  • 3 = three AI reviewer personas (not one voice):
    • The Fighter Scientist — trusts full-contact proof; distrusts awareness seminars without sparring
    • The Police/Data Pragmatist — trusts government victim surveys and police outcomes; distrusts gym marketing
    • The Hard Skeptic — trusts medical trials only; calls out missing data and myths (like “90% of fights go to the ground”)
  • 7 = seven major approaches compared (EAAA, MMA, BJJ, boxing/wrestling, Krav, traditional MA, military awareness doctrine)
  • 7 questions = each approach scored 0–10 on seven evidence tests (avoidance, multi-attacker escape, proof, fighting skill, control, live training, did victimization actually drop?)

Three waves — how one round works:

  1. Wave 1 — Score alone. Each reviewer fills the grid from sources only. No negotiating yet.
  2. Wave 2 — Attack the scores. Reviewers challenge each other (“You gave MMA a 9 on avoidance — show me the RCT.” “You capped EAAA on punching — it still has the only victimization trial.”).
  3. Wave 3 — Revise & lock in. Scores adjust. Everyone must accept data gaps openly. Weighted math produces final rankings. Unanimous agreements become the five findings below.

How this gets you the right data, not the loudest data:

  • A boxing coach cannot win by cheering boxing — the skeptic blocks uncited claims.
  • A awareness-only advocate cannot win — the fighter blocks skills that were never pressure-tested.
  • What ranks high must please avoidance + real proof (weighted highest) — not Instagram toughness.
  • Marketing that sounds good but has no study survives Wave 2 and gets cut (Krav, traditional point-karate).
  • Honest unknowns stay labeled unknown (e.g. “I left before the fight” — still not scientifically measured).

What this is not: Not a clinical trial. Not one AI’s hot take. It is a bias-canceling filter over existing evidence — so rankings reflect what survived argument, not what sells memberships.

Strategies, Tactics & Technique Systems

Self-defense is not one skill — it is a stack. At the top: why you act (strategy). In the middle: when and how you act (tactics). At the bottom: the trained bundles gyms teach (technique systems). Individual punches and kicks sit below that.

AI NHI — Plain English hierarchy

Think of it like a pyramid, not a martial art belt

Strategy = your win condition. "Never enter the fight" beats "submit them on the ground" for ordinary civilian life — and the EAAA medical trial is the only thing with gold-standard proof at that level.

Tactics = mid-level moves tied to a moment in the encounter. Pre-contact tactics (spot danger, plan exits) have the strongest evidence. Contact tactics (strike bursts, sprawls) matter when you are already engaged. Post-contact tactics (stand up, run to a crowd) decide whether you leave intact.

Technique systems = what you actually train — EAAA, Cooper's color codes, boxing, BJJ, MMA, Krav Maga. Each system bundles techniques under a strategy. A boxing gym teaches standup-finish strategy whether the coach says so or not. A sport BJJ gym teaches combat-dominance — useful in a ring, risky defaults on concrete.

Individual techniques (covered in the next section) are the smallest pieces — a jab, a calf kick, a rear naked choke. Evidence is strongest at the top of this pyramid and weakest at the bottom for civilian survival.

Techniques — Punches, Kicks, and Grappling in Plain English

The style name on the door (karate, boxing, kung fu) matters less than whether you practice against someone who hits back. Here is what the physics and fight footage say.

AI NHI — Technique insights

Striking: why boxing beats most traditional punches

A karate reverse punch and a boxing cross use similar hip rotation. Lab tests show both can hit around 2,500 newtons of force. The difference: boxers throw thousands of those punches at people who punch back. Most kung fu and point-karate schools never do. The delivery system matters more than the fist shape.

Best punches (when you must strike):

  • Jab — short, fast, blinds and measures distance. Rarely ends the fight alone but sets everything up.
  • Cross — rear-hand straight — main knockout tool in street footage.
  • Hook — around the guard to the jaw — highest concussion risk.
  • Palm heel — same idea as a punch but safer for your hand on a hard skull.

Best kicks: low calf kick (destroys their ability to chase you), push kick (creates distance). High spinning movie kicks — almost never seen working in 154 real fights.

Best grappling: wrestling double-leg takedown and getting behind someone (rear choke) dominate sport fighting. On the street: sprawl to stop takedowns, stand up fast if you fall. Pulling someone into your guard on concrete is a bad bet.

Stance, Footwork, Guard & Weapon Response

Before you throw anything, how you stand and present your body decides whether you can leave, absorb a shot, or survive until help arrives. When a weapon appears, the rules change completely.

AI NHI — Plain English

Posture is for exit, not ego

A UFC fighter squares up to trade in a cage. On a sidewalk, your stance should answer one question: can I run in any direction right now? Hands protect your jaw. Chin stays tucked. Feet stay under you on bad ground. None of this requires years of training — it requires stopping the movie-pose habit.

When a weapon is shown: roughly 3 in 4 U.S. murders involve guns. Compliance and distance beat disarm drills every time in the evidence we have. Krav Maga weapon defenses score Tier C–D because nobody has measured real civilian disarm success. Treat every weapon as real. Give property. Exit when the window opens.

Stance — Exit-Ready, Not Squared Up

Blade your body 45°. Lead foot forward, weight on the balls of your feet, knees soft. You are ready to angle off or sprint — not pose for a round.

Footwork — Angle Off, Don't Shuffle Backward

Straight retreat lets a motivated attacker close faster. Step 45° offline to open an escape lane. Never cross your feet — tangled legs on concrete end fights.

Hands & Arms — Compact Guard

Hands at cheekbone height, elbows protecting ribs. Palms-out signals de-escalation and keeps palm-heel ready. Do not reach out to block — that pulls you into clinch range.

Head & Vitals — What to Hide First

One clean shot to an untucked chin ends most encounters. Protect brain → throat → ribs → groin, in that order. Eyes watch their chest and hands, not the ground.

Only if you accept Tier D evidence. Cooperative Krav drills look convincing on video, but no peer-reviewed study measures civilian disarm success against a resisting, armed attacker. Inside close range, reaction time favors the person already holding the weapon. Police and military train weapon retention for their gun — not taking yours from a robber's hand. Comply, exit, report. That is the A-tier protocol.

"What about chin down — can't I see less?"

Your eyes track from their chest and shoulders — you see hand movement earlier than staring at their face. Chin tuck protects the knockout button. Boxers do this for a reason; street fights end the same way.

Fight Science — Hard Numbers & Physics

Force in newtons, knockouts in G-forces, reaction time in milliseconds, ground probability in percentages. This is the biomechanics and outcome data behind every ranking on this site.

AI NHI — Fight science in plain English

Physics does not care about your belt color

A karate reverse punch and a boxing cross both measure around 2,500 newtons in the lab. The difference is effective mass delivered under stress against someone hitting back — that is the kinetic chain, not fist shape. Knockouts are rapid head acceleration (brainstem shear), not simply "hard enough." Hooks and falls rotate the brain worse than straight shots.

64% of street knockouts land in the first 10 seconds. Choice reaction time runs 400–600ms+ — inside punching range, the person who moves first wins unless you already acted (leaving, hands up, pre-committed exit). Ground happens in 62–73% of fights, but only 41% get back up. The 90% ground myth is debunked LAPD data.

Six Fight Science Principles

"If karate hits as hard as boxing in the lab, why does boxing win on the street?"

Because fight science measures two different things: peak force in newtons (lab) and delivery under live resistance (fights). The kinetic chain — foot drive, hip rotation, effective mass — only fully wires under stress sparring. A 2,645N karate punch that never lands on a resisting opponent is a physics demo, not a fight skill.

"How hard do you have to hit to knock someone out?"

Combat sports TBI research cites roughly 70–100G linear head acceleration and high rotational acceleration for knockouts — but the practical answer from street footage is simpler: clean shots to the jaw in the first 10 seconds end most fights. After that, exhaustion, grappling, and third parties change everything.

Types of Violence — Why UFC Is Not the Street

Training for the wrong type of violence is like studying chess to win a street argument. Each level below is a different game with different rules.

Plain English

Martial arts class — cooperative partners, no weapons, rules, stop when it hurts.
UFC — agreed fight, matched size, referee, doctor, no weapons, both want to be there.
Street fight — often ego, friends watching, maybe no rules — but still usually one-on-one.
Real predatory violence — surprise, someone you may know, weapons, multiple people, you did not agree to any of it.
